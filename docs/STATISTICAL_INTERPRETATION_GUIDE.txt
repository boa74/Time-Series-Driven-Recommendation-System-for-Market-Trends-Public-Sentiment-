1. STATISTICAL SIGNIFICANCE (P-Value)
====================================================================================================

The p-value tells us the probability that the observed correlation occurred by chance.

Lower p-value = More confident the relationship is real

Standard thresholds:
  • p < 0.001 (***): Less than 0.1% chance this is random → HIGHLY RELIABLE
  • p < 0.01  (**):  Less than 1% chance this is random → VERY RELIABLE
  • p < 0.05  (*):   Less than 5% chance this is random → RELIABLE
  • p ≥ 0.05  (ns):  More than 5% chance this is random → UNRELIABLE

====================================================================================================
2. EFFECT SIZE (Correlation Magnitude)
====================================================================================================

Even if statistically significant, is the effect large enough to matter?

Cohen's guidelines for correlation strength:
  • |r| < 0.10:  Negligible → Practically meaningless
  • |r| 0.10-0.29: Small → Detectable but weak relationship
  • |r| 0.30-0.49: Medium → Moderate, meaningful relationship
  • |r| ≥ 0.50:  Large → Strong, important relationship

3. CONFIDENCE INTERVALS
====================================================================================================

95% Confidence Interval shows the range where true correlation likely falls.

Narrow CI = Precise estimate
Wide CI = Uncertain estimate
CI containing 0 = Might be no relationship at all

4. SAMPLE SIZE (N)
====================================================================================================

Larger sample = More reliable results
Smaller sample = Higher chance of false positives/negatives

Our analysis has N = 551 for most correlations
With N > 30, results are generally reliable if p < 0.05
